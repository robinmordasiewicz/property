      How service providers can help prevent video streams from cannibalising network capacity

      • Report this post
      Bart Salaets

      Bart Salaets

      Published Mar 31, 2020
      + Follow

      Video streaming services are battling for bandwidth with a growing army of home workers. 

      Last week, the EU and other nation states even urged the world’s top video providers to reduce streaming quality in order to help service providers cope with intensifying network strains.

      While the OTT video giants’ collective response has already had a significant and positive impact, it has highlighted an enduring problem that needs to be addressed when everything settles down.

      Currently, most online video providers use Adaptive Bitrate (ABR) video technology, which adjusts video quality to suit available network capacity. It usually works very well. 

      Unfortunately, ABR technology can sometimes cause more congestion problems, particularly during periods of heavy traffic. The worst-case scenario is that that any newly added – and increasingly vital – network capacity is automatically monopolised by video clients attempting to achieve higher bitrates and video stream quality.

      Looking ahead, the onus is now on service providers to ensure that similar video and ABR-related network issues do not occur in a post-COVID-19 world. Many wireline networks recently got a capacity upgrade so bandwidth problems are unlikely to occur soon in that part of the network. But mobile networks are more capacity constrained and are therefore more likely to hit capacity limits in the future due to increased mobile video consumption.

      The good news is that technology already exists that can identify and cap ABR-encoded video flow bandwidth with policies. 

      Importantly, this means that service providers are free to define how, when, and by how much, they want to enforce bandwidth control policies. Additional, granular policy customisation is also possible using criteria such as time, congestion levels, device type (screen size) and subscriber personas.
